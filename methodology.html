Executive Summary

What this calculator is and who it is for

The KOMBIT Value Realization Calculator is an interactive financial model designed to estimate the annual cost savings achievable through Testing as a Service (TaaS) combined with test automation, deployed across Danish municipal IT projects.

It is designed for three audiences: executive leadership (CFOs, IT Directors) who need a financial justification; Test Managers and Test Architects who need an auditable breakdown; and external stakeholders (OpenText, KL, municipal representatives) who need to understand the methodology behind the numbers.

Purpose & Scope

What the calculator does and does not do

What it DOES
  • ✓Estimates gross financial gain from automation across municipalities
  • ✓Calculates per-role, per-person, per-release savings
  • ✓Converts hours saved to days, weeks, and sprint equivalents
  • ✓Shows net savings after deducting automation infrastructure cost
  • ✓Provides breakeven guidance when net is negative
  • ✓Assumes 100% tester utilisation (all hours are billable)
What it DOES NOT do
  • ✗Does not measure quality improvements or defect prevention value
  • ✗Does not account for risk reduction or compliance benefits
  • ✗Does not model partial utilisation or over-capacity scenarios
  • ✗Does not account for staff redeployment value
  • ✗Does not model the compounding value of faster releases
  • ✗Does not replace a full business case analysis

Formula — Plain English

Step by step in plain language — no prior knowledge required

1Start with your baseline hours. You enter how many manual testing hours one release takes. This is your starting point — the total effort before any automation exists.
2Adjust for release complexity. A simple release takes less effort than your baseline suggests; a complex release takes more. The calculator scales your hours by 0.7×, 1.0×, or 1.5× accordingly.
3Split hours across three roles. 65% of hours belong to Manual Testers, 25% to Automation Engineers, and 10% to Test Managers. Each role has its own hourly rate.
4Apply automation gain — differently per role. Automation eliminates 100% of recoverable manual tester hours, but only 30% of automation engineer hours (they still build scripts), and only 5% of manager hours (oversight does not go away).
5Multiply across releases and municipalities. The savings per release are multiplied by releases per year and the number of municipalities to give the annual total.
6Convert hours to money. Each role's saved hours are multiplied by that role's hourly rate. The three totals are summed to produce the Gross Financial Gain.
7Subtract automation costs to get net savings. The annual automation cost per municipality is multiplied by municipalities and subtracted from gross gain. The result can be positive or negative — both shown honestly.
8Convert hours to days and weeks for operational context. Total hours ÷ working hours per day = person-days. Days per release ÷ 5 = weeks earlier. Person-days ÷ 10 = sprint equivalents recovered.

Formula — Mathematical Notation

For technical audiences and audit purposes

Step 1 — Complexity Adjustment
// Input: ManualHours (slider) × ComplexityMultiplier (lookup) AdjustedHours = ManualHours × ComplexityMultiplier // ComplexityMultiplier lookup: // Simple = 0.7 | Medium = 1.0 | Complex = 1.5
Step 2 — Split Hours By Role
// Fixed distribution — see Section 7 for full rationale ManualRoleHours = AdjustedHours × 0.65 // 65% — manual execution AutoRoleHours = AdjustedHours × 0.25 // 25% — automation engineering MgmtRoleHours = AdjustedHours × 0.10 // 10% — test management
Step 3 — Hours Recovered Per Role (per release, per municipality)
// AutomationGain × role-specific reduction factor prevents overestimation ManualRecovered = ManualRoleHours × AutomationGain × 1.0 // fully replaced by automation AutoRecovered = AutoRoleHours × AutomationGain × 0.3 // still maintain scripts MgmtRecovered = MgmtRoleHours × AutomationGain × 0.05 // oversight remains
Step 4 — Annual Total Hours Recovered
TotalHrsRecovered = (ManualRecovered + AutoRecovered + MgmtRecovered) × Municipalities × ReleasesPerYear
Step 5 — Gross Financial Gain
// Each role's hours × its own rate, summed, then scaled GrossGain = (ManualRecovered × RateManual + AutoRecovered × RateAuto + MgmtRecovered × RateMgmt) × Municipalities × ReleasesPerYear
Step 6 — Net Savings (honest — no zero floor)
TotalAutoCost = AutoCostPerMuniPerYear × Municipalities NetSavings = GrossGain - TotalAutoCost // NetSavings CAN be negative — shown honestly in red in the calculator
Step 7 — Time Conversions (hours → days → weeks → sprints)
PersonDays = TotalHrsRecovered ÷ WorkingHrsPerDay SprintEquivalents = PersonDays ÷ 10 // 1 sprint = 10 working days DaysPerRelease = (ManualRecovered + AutoRecovered + MgmtRecovered) ÷ WorkingHrsPerDay WeeksEarlier = DaysPerRelease ÷ 5 // 5 working days per week
Step 8 — Delivery Cycle Reduction (dampened)
CycleReduction = AutomationGain × 0.4 // 0.4 dampening: planning, reviews, deployment, approvals remain unchanged // Example: 50% automation gain → only 20% cycle reduction
Step 9 — Per-Person Breakdown (presentation only — totals unchanged)
HrsPerManualTester = (ManualRecovered × ReleasesPerYear) ÷ ManualTesterCount HrsPerAutoEng = (AutoRecovered × ReleasesPerYear) ÷ AutoEngCount HrsPerMgr = (MgmtRecovered × ReleasesPerYear) ÷ MgrCount // ÷ headcount × headcount cancels at 100% utilisation // GrossGain is IDENTICAL regardless of headcount entered // These exist for auditability and per-person communication only

Hardcoded Assumptions

Every fixed value used in the formula — what it is, why it was chosen, and what changing it would mean

Role Hour Distribution
Manual Tester Share
65%
Based on typical QE team composition in public sector IT. Manual testers perform the bulk of execution work. Industry references suggest 60–70% for early-to-mid automation maturity.
Auto Engineer Share
25%
Automation engineers spend time building frameworks, writing scripts, and maintaining pipelines. At 50% maturity, approximately one quarter of total effort is in this role.
Test Manager Share
10%
Test management covers planning, coordination, reporting, and governance. Relatively constant regardless of automation level, typically 8–12% of total effort.
Automation Reduction Factors Per Role
Manual Tester
1.0×
Automation directly replaces manual execution. Every hour of automated coverage removes one manual tester hour. Full gain applies.
Automation Engineer
0.3×
Engineers still spend most time building and maintaining the automation that produces the savings. Only 30% of their effort is freed. Ignoring this would significantly overstate savings.
Test Manager
0.05×
Management overhead — planning, governance, compliance — is largely independent of automation. Only 5% reduces. Conservative and intentional to avoid inflating the model.
Complexity Multipliers
Simple
0.7×
A simple release requires approximately 70% of medium effort. Fewer test cases, less regression depth. The 30% reduction is a calibration assumption — replace with your own historical data for higher accuracy.
Medium
1.0×
The baseline. The hours you entered are assumed to represent a typical medium-complexity release. No adjustment is made. Enter your most common release type's hours as the baseline.
Complex
1.5×
A complex release requires 50% more effort than medium. The asymmetry is intentional — complexity adds more testing pain than simplicity saves, reflecting real-world QE experience.
Other Fixed Values
Cycle Dampening
0.4×
Automation gain does not translate 1:1 to delivery cycle reduction. Planning, reviews, deployment gates, and approval processes remain unchanged. 50% automation gain = only 20% cycle reduction.
Sprint Length
10 days
Standard two-week sprint = 10 working days. Used to convert person-days to sprint equivalents for agile teams.
Utilisation
100%
All testers are assumed fully utilised — every hour is billable. Valid for KOMBIT's TaaS model. For organisations with permanent employees where hours saved do not reduce cost directly, the gross gain would be overstated.

Hours vs Days — Why Both Matter

The same number, told in the language of each stakeholder

Hours are the unit of financial calculation — they convert directly to money. Days and weeks are the unit of operational planning — they communicate what the saving actually means to a team, a project, or a release calendar.

For CFOs
1,752 hrs
Converts to money. This is what gets signed off.
For Project Managers
219 days
Capacity freed for higher-value work. This is what gets planned.
For Scrum Teams
21.9 sprints
Equivalent capacity recovered. This is what feels real.
Conversion Chain
Total Hrs Recovered ÷ Working Hrs/Day = Person-Days
Person-Days ÷ 10 = Sprint Equivalents
Hrs per Release per Muni ÷ Working Hrs/Day = Days Earlier per Release
Days Earlier per Release ÷ 5 = Weeks Earlier Delivery

How To Interpret Results

What each output means and how to act on it

✓Positive Net Savings

The automation investment pays for itself from cost savings alone. Use directly in a business case for investment approval.

−Negative Net Savings

Does NOT mean ValueEdge has no value. It means current slider configuration needs calibration:

  • →Automation cost per municipality is set too high — reduce to reflect shared infrastructure
  • →Testing volume is set too low — increase to reflect actual effort
  • →Automation gain is too conservative — mature frameworks achieve 70–85%
  • →Number of municipalities is low — more municipalities spread the automation cost
iPer-Person Breakdown

Does not change the total numbers — redistributes the total across the team for auditability and role-level communication.

⚡Delivery Cycle Reduction

Dampened by 0.4 to reflect real-world constraints. Days and weeks translate the percentage into operational language.

What This Calculator Does Not Measure

The financial model captures cost savings only — real value is significantly higher

These numbers represent a conservative lower bound. The following value dimensions are real but not quantified here:

🎯
Quality Improvements
Fewer defects, earlier detection, higher coverage — not captured. Cost of a production defect in a municipal system is significant.
🛡
Risk Reduction
Automated regression prevents regression defects. Avoided incident costs not in this model.
⚖️
Compliance Value
Automated audit trails, traceability, and documented test evidence have compliance value not captured here.
👥
Staff Redeployment
Hours freed shift to exploratory testing, test design, requirement review. Productivity uplift not in this model.
📈
Compounding Release Value
1.1 weeks earlier per release × 4 releases per year. Citizen and organisational value of earlier delivery not captured.
🧠
Knowledge Retention
Automated tests are living documentation surviving staff turnover. Cost of knowledge loss not in this model.
🤖
Aviator AI Multiplier
OpenText Aviator AI saves additional 45 minutes to 2 hours per task on test creation, defect summarisation, and requirement analysis. This AI productivity layer is separate from automation gain and adds significant additional value.

Glossary

Every term used in the calculator and this document, defined

TaaS
Testing as a Service. A delivery model where testing expertise, processes, and tools are provided as a managed service. KOMBIT uses TaaS to flex testing capacity across 98 municipalities without maintaining a permanent in-house testing team for each.
CSDP / ValueEdge
Core Software Delivery Platform. OpenText's unified platform for managing the full quality lifecycle — requirements, test planning, execution, defect tracking, and reporting.
Automation Gain
The percentage of testing effort eliminated by automation. Applied differently per role — Manual 1.0×, Auto Engineer 0.3×, Test Manager 0.05×.
Gross Gain
Total financial saving before costs. Hours recovered × hourly rates × releases × municipalities.
Net Savings
Gross Gain minus automation cost. Can be negative — shown honestly without a zero floor.
Role Distribution
Assumed split of total testing hours: Manual Tester 65%, Automation Engineer 25%, Test Manager 10%.
Reduction Factor
Per-role multiplier on automation gain to prevent overestimation: Manual 1.0, Auto Engineer 0.3, Test Manager 0.05.
Complexity Multiplier
Factor applied to baseline hours: Simple ×0.7, Medium ×1.0, Complex ×1.5.
Cycle Dampening
Fixed factor of 0.4 applied to automation gain for delivery cycle reduction. Reflects non-testing activities that remain unchanged.
Person-Days
Total hours recovered ÷ working hours per day. One person's full working day of recovered capacity.
Sprint Equivalent
Person-days ÷ 10. Expresses recovered capacity in agile planning terms.
Breakeven
Point where Gross Gain equals Total Automation Cost. Net Savings = €0.
Fully Utilised
All testers working at 100% capacity. Headcount inputs affect only the per-person breakdown display, not the total financial gain.
