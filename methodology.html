Executive Summary

What this calculator is and who it is for

The KOMBIT Value Realization Calculator is an interactive financial model designed to estimate the annual cost savings achievable through Testing as a Service (TaaS) combined with test automation, deployed across Danish municipal IT projects.

It is designed for three audiences: executive leadership (CFOs, IT Directors) who need a financial justification; Test Managers and Test Architects who need an auditable breakdown; and external stakeholders (OpenText, KL, municipal representatives) who need to understand the methodology behind the numbers.

Purpose & Scope

What the calculator does and does not do

What it DOES
  • ✓Estimates gross financial gain from automation across municipalities
  • ✓Calculates per-role, per-person, per-release savings
  • ✓Converts hours saved to days, weeks, and sprint equivalents
  • ✓Shows net savings after deducting automation infrastructure cost
  • ✓Provides breakeven guidance when net is negative
  • ✓Assumes 100% tester utilisation (all hours are billable)
What it DOES NOT do
  • ✗Does not measure quality improvements or defect prevention value
  • ✗Does not account for risk reduction or compliance benefits
  • ✗Does not model partial utilisation or over-capacity scenarios
  • ✗Does not account for staff redeployment value
  • ✗Does not model the compounding value of faster releases
  • ✗Does not replace a full business case analysis

Formula — Plain English

Step by step in plain language — no prior knowledge required

1Start with your baseline hours. You enter how many manual testing hours one release takes. This is your starting point — the total effort before any automation exists.
2Adjust for release complexity. A simple release takes less effort than your baseline suggests; a complex release takes more. The calculator scales your hours by 0.7×, 1.0×, or 1.5× accordingly.
3Split hours across three roles. Not everyone on the team does the same work. 65% of hours belong to Manual Testers, 25% to Automation Engineers, and 10% to Test Managers. Each role has its own hourly rate.
4Apply automation gain — differently per role. Automation eliminates 100% of recoverable manual tester hours, but only 30% of automation engineer hours (they still build and maintain scripts), and only 5% of manager hours (oversight does not go away). This prevents overestimating savings.
5Multiply across releases and municipalities. The savings per release are multiplied by the number of releases per year and the number of municipalities. This gives you the annual total across the entire KOMBIT ecosystem.
6Convert hours to money. Each role's saved hours are multiplied by that role's hourly rate. The three totals are summed to produce the Gross Financial Gain — what you would save if automation had no cost.
7Subtract automation costs to get net savings. The annual automation cost per municipality (tooling, infrastructure, maintenance) is multiplied by the number of municipalities and subtracted from the gross gain. The result can be positive or negative — both are shown honestly.
8Convert hours to days and weeks for operational context. Total hours are divided by your working hours per day to produce person-days. Days per release are divided by 5 to show weeks earlier delivery. Person-days are divided by 10 (one sprint) to show sprint equivalents recovered.

Hardcoded Assumptions

Every fixed value used in the formula — what it is, why it was chosen, and what changing it would mean

Role Hour Distribution
Manual Tester Share
65%
Based on typical QE team composition in public sector IT projects. Manual testers perform the bulk of execution work — functional testing, regression, exploratory. Industry references (ISTQB, Gartner) suggest 60–70% for organisations at early-to-mid automation maturity.
Automation Engineer Share
25%
Automation engineers spend time building frameworks, writing scripts, maintaining pipelines, and reviewing failures. At 50% automation maturity, approximately one-quarter of total effort is in this role.
Test Manager Share
10%
Test management covers planning, coordination, reporting, stakeholder communication and governance. This overhead is relatively constant regardless of automation level, typically 8–12% of total effort.
Automation Reduction Factors Per Role
Manual Tester
1.0×
Automation directly replaces manual test execution. Every hour of automated test coverage directly removes one manual tester hour. This is the role automation was designed to replace, so the full gain applies.
Automation Engineer
0.3×
Automation engineers still spend most of their time building and maintaining the automation that produces the savings. Only 30% of their effort is freed — the overhead of creating and maintaining tests does not disappear, it shifts. Ignoring this would significantly overstate savings.
Test Manager
0.05×
Management overhead — planning, governance, stakeholder reporting, compliance — is largely independent of whether tests are manual or automated. Only 5% of management time is estimated to reduce. This is a conservative and intentional choice to avoid inflating the model.
Complexity Multipliers
Simple
0.7×
A simple release (patch, minor update, single module change) requires approximately 70% of the effort of a medium release. Fewer test cases, less regression depth, smaller scope. The 30% reduction is a calibration assumption — replace with your own historical data for higher accuracy.
Medium
1.0×
Medium is the baseline — it means the hours you entered are assumed to be representative of a typical medium-complexity release. No adjustment is made. This is why you should enter your most common release type's hours as the baseline.
Complex
1.5×
A complex release (major integration, system overhaul, cross-municipality scope) requires 50% more effort than a medium release. This asymmetry is intentional — complexity adds more testing pain than simplicity saves, reflecting real-world QE experience.
Other Fixed Values
Cycle Dampening
0.4×
Automation gain does not translate 1:1 to delivery cycle reduction. Planning, reviews, deployment gates, and approval processes remain unchanged. At 50% automation gain, the actual cycle reduction is approximately 20% (50% × 0.4). This prevents the dashboard from claiming unrealistic speed improvements.
Sprint Length
10 days
A standard two-week sprint = 10 working days. Used to convert person-days to sprint equivalents for agile teams. Adjust your working hours per day slider if your sprints run on a different working-day calendar.
Utilisation
100%
All testers are assumed to be fully utilised — every hour entered is a billable hour. This is valid for KOMBIT's TaaS model where consultants are engaged per-project. For organisations with permanent employees where hours saved do not reduce cost directly, the gross gain would be overstated.

Hours vs Days — Why Both Matter

The same number, told in the language of each stakeholder

Hours are the unit of financial calculation — they convert directly to money. Days and weeks are the unit of operational planning — they communicate what the saving actually means to a team, a project, or a release calendar.

For CFOs
1,752 hrs
Converts to money. This is what gets signed off.
For Project Managers
219 days
Capacity freed for higher-value work. This is what gets planned.
For Scrum Teams
21.9 sprints
Equivalent capacity recovered. This is what feels real.
Conversion Chain
Total Hrs Recovered ÷ Working Hrs/Day = Person-Days
Person-Days ÷ 10 = Sprint Equivalents
Hrs per Release per Muni ÷ Working Hrs/Day = Days Earlier per Release
Days Earlier per Release ÷ 5 = Weeks Earlier Delivery

How To Interpret Results

What each output means and how to act on it

✓Positive Net Savings

The automation investment pays for itself from cost savings alone. The higher the number, the faster the payback. This can be used directly in a business case for automation investment approval.

−Negative Net Savings

The automation cost in the current slider configuration exceeds the gross savings. This does NOT mean ValueEdge or automation has no value. It means one or more of the following:

  • →The automation cost per municipality is set too high — reduce it to reflect shared infrastructure
  • →The testing volume (hours/releases) is set too low — increase to reflect actual effort
  • →The automation gain is set too conservatively — mature frameworks achieve 70–85%
  • →The number of municipalities is low — more municipalities spread the automation cost
iPer-Person Breakdown

The per-person breakdown does not change the total numbers — it redistributes the total across the team. Use it to communicate to each role what automation means for their workload: a manual tester saving 31 hours per year understands the impact better than an abstract total of 1,752 hours.

⚡Delivery Cycle Reduction

The cycle reduction percentage is dampened by 0.4 to reflect real-world constraints. A 50% automation gain does not produce a 50% faster release — planning, approval, and deployment remain unchanged. The days and weeks figures translate the percentage into operational language.

What This Calculator Does Not Measure

The financial model captures cost savings only — real value is significantly higher

The numbers produced by this calculator represent a conservative lower bound of the total value of automation. The following value dimensions are real but not quantified here:

🎯
Quality Improvements
Fewer defects reaching production, earlier defect detection, higher test coverage — none of these are captured. The cost of a production defect in a municipal system serving citizens is significant.
🛡
Risk Reduction
Automated regression prevents regression defects on every release. The avoided cost of a regression incident — downtime, citizen impact, emergency fix — is not in this model.
⚖️
Compliance Value
KOMBIT operates under strict public sector compliance requirements. Automated audit trails, traceability from requirement to test execution, and documented test evidence have compliance value not captured here.
👥
Staff Redeployment
Hours freed from manual testing are not destroyed — they are redeployed. Testers shift to exploratory testing, test design, requirement review, and higher-value activities. This productivity uplift is not in this model.
📈
Compounding Release Value
Releasing 1.1 weeks earlier per release, compounded across 4 releases, means features and fixes reach citizens significantly earlier each year. The citizen and organisational value of earlier delivery is not captured.
🧠
Knowledge Retention
Automated tests are living documentation. They encode system knowledge that survives staff turnover. The cost of knowledge loss when a manual tester leaves is not in this model.
🤖
Aviator AI Multiplier
The integration of OpenText Aviator AI further accelerates test creation, defect summarization, and requirement analysis — saving an additional 45 minutes to 2 hours per task. This AI productivity layer is separate from the automation gain modelled here and adds significant additional value.

Glossary

Every term used in the calculator and this document, defined

TaaS
Testing as a Service. A delivery model where testing expertise, processes, and tools are provided as a managed service, typically by an external vendor. KOMBIT uses TaaS to flex testing capacity across 98 municipalities without maintaining a permanent in-house testing team for each.
CSDP / ValueEdge
Core Software Delivery Platform. OpenText's unified platform for managing the full quality lifecycle — requirements, test planning, execution, defect tracking, and reporting. Referred to as ValueEdge in its current product generation.
Automation Gain
The percentage of total testing effort eliminated through automated test execution. A 50% automation gain means half the manual testing work is replaced by scripts that run automatically. Applied differently per role — see Assumptions section.
Gross Gain
The total financial saving from automation before any costs are deducted. Calculated from hours recovered × hourly rates × releases × municipalities. Represents the theoretical maximum saving if automation had no cost.
Net Savings
Gross Gain minus the annual automation infrastructure cost. Can be negative — this does not indicate failure, it indicates that the current cost structure needs optimisation. The calculator shows the real number honestly.
Role Distribution
The assumed split of total testing hours across the three roles: Manual Tester (65%), Automation Engineer (25%), Test Manager (10%). A hardcoded assumption reflecting typical QE team structures in public sector IT.
Reduction Factor
A per-role multiplier applied to the automation gain to prevent overestimation. Manual testers: 1.0 (full reduction). Automation engineers: 0.3 (they still maintain scripts). Test managers: 0.05 (oversight remains). Without these factors, the model would claim every automation gain is equally distributed.
Complexity Multiplier
A factor applied to baseline hours to reflect release complexity: Simple ×0.7, Medium ×1.0, Complex ×1.5. Allows the calculator to model different release types without requiring separate input for each.
Cycle Dampening
A fixed factor of 0.4 applied to automation gain when calculating delivery cycle reduction. Reflects the reality that not all release activities benefit from test automation — planning, reviews, deployment, and approval gates remain. A 50% automation gain yields only a 20% cycle reduction.
Person-Days
Total hours recovered divided by working hours per day. One person-day represents one team member's full working day worth of effort recovered. A more intuitive unit than hours for capacity planning discussions.
Sprint Equivalent
Total person-days divided by 10 (one standard two-week sprint). Expresses recovered capacity in agile planning terms. "21.9 sprint equivalents" is more meaningful to an agile team than "219 person-days."
Breakeven
The point at which Gross Gain equals Total Automation Cost, producing a Net Savings of zero. The calculator provides breakeven guidance when net savings are negative, showing which sliders to adjust to reach this point.
Fully Utilised
The assumption that all testers are working at 100% capacity — every hour is billable. This means headcount inputs only affect the per-person breakdown display, not the total financial gain. Valid for TaaS consultant models.
